Every team that tracks equipment runs the same operation. They acquire something physical, they assign it to a person, they let it move, they try to get it back. The variations are in scale, vocabulary, and what happens when the loop breaks.

For the past year, we watched that loop run inside thousands of workspaces on Shelf. This report is a return of that observation — anonymized, aggregated, and given back to the industry for free.

The headline finding is simple. **Most teams are tracking more equipment than they have an accountability story for.** Custody coverage is patchy, audits are rarer than they should be, and a meaningful percentage of every inventory is missing the moment you check.

The details are below.

## Methodology

We believe a report is only as good as its methodology. Here is the full disclosure:

**Data source.** Anonymized telemetry from production Shelf workspaces. No customer-identifying data was used. All numbers in this report are aggregates over cohorts of at least 20 workspaces; cohorts smaller than 20 are not reported.

**Time window.** The 12 months ending **April 30, 2026** (data window: 2025-05-01 through 2026-04-30).

**Sample.** Approximately **{{TODO: workspaces}} workspaces**, **{{TODO: assets}} assets**, spanning **{{TODO: countries}} countries**. Inclusion criteria below.

**Inclusion criteria.** A workspace is included in the report if it meets all of the following:
- Workspace type is **Team** (Personal workspaces are excluded — they are individual sandboxes that don't reflect organizational behavior).
- Workspace is **not disabled** at the end of the window.
- The owning user is **not deleted**.
- The workspace has tracked **at least 10 assets** during the window (workspaces below this threshold typically reflect trial / evaluation activity).
- The workspace is **not on a known internal / staff allowlist** (Shelf employees, demo workspaces, support workspaces are excluded).
- Where a stat requires a specific feature (e.g. audits, bookings), the workspace must have that feature enabled.

**Anonymization.** No customer names, workspace names, user names, asset titles, or location names appear in any aggregate. Numerical aggregates are rounded to one significant figure (e.g. 3,047 workspaces is published as "approximately 3,000"). Quotes attributed to specific customers in this report are published with that customer's explicit permission.

**Definitions.** Where a stat could be defined multiple ways, we picked the most conservative definition.
- **Active custody** means an asset has a `Custody` row (current custodian) at the moment of measurement. Historical custody transfers are tracked via `ActivityEvent` and used for handover-rate calculations.
- **Ghost asset** is an asset that was marked Missing in two or more consecutive audits within the window AND had no scan activity between those audits.
- **Idle asset** is an asset with no activity events (scan, custody change, booking, location update) in the prior 90 days.
- **Booking conflict averted** is an attempted booking creation that failed the conflict-prevention check.
- **Audit completion duration** is `completedAt - startedAt`, measured for audits that reached the Completed status within the window.

**Confidence levels.** Each stat in this report carries a confidence level:
- **High** — derived from a comprehensive telemetry source covering the entire eligible cohort.
- **Medium** — derived from a partial telemetry source (e.g. opt-in feature usage) or a definition that involves a judgement call.
- **Low** — reported with the figure but flagged in copy; do not cite without checking the underlying definition.

**Methodology version.** This methodology is published as version 1.0. Subsequent annual editions of this report will track diffs in methodology version.

**Reproducibility.** The extraction script that produced these aggregates is published in the open at [github.com/Shelf-nu/shelf.nu](https://github.com/Shelf-nu/shelf.nu) under `apps/webapp/scripts/state-of-em-2026/`. The script does not include customer data — only the queries and aggregation logic.

**Limitations.** Self-hosted Shelf instances run on customer-owned databases and are not included in this dataset — the report reflects hosted-cloud usage only. Self-hosted patterns may differ. Adoption of the bookings and audits features is not uniform; stats restricted to feature-enabled workspaces are noted as such. "Missing" in an audit context is what was scanned-or-not-scanned during a specific audit session — it is not a permanent property of the asset. We use it to size operational gaps, not to imply asset destruction.

Physical audits are the only mechanism that reconciles digital records against physical reality. They are also the operation that teams skip first when time is tight.

The **{{TODO: au_pct_audited_assets_missing}}%** Missing rate is the single most important number in this section. It means roughly one in every **{{TODO: 100/missing_rate}}** items the records claim is in a location was not actually in that location at the moment of audit.

Three caveats:
- An asset can be Missing because it has moved without an update, not because it has been lost. The next scan in another location resolves it.
- The Missing rate is heavily concentrated in specific categories — small portable items, kit components, consumable-adjacent items.
- Workspaces that audit on a regular cadence have lower Missing rates over time. Audits surface the problem, and the problem then gets fixed; teams that don't audit don't observe the gap.

The **{{TODO: au_pct_audited_assets_unexpected}}%** Unexpected rate is the other half of the equation. These are assets that were scanned during an audit but were not on the expected list — items that have wandered into the audited location from somewhere else. Unexpected scans are how teams discover that the inventory of a given room has drifted from what the system thinks.

## Finding 5: The cost of disorder

The true cost of equipment mismanagement is not the obviously-lost item. It is the slow accumulation of ghost assets, the long tail of idle gear, and the time spent recovering things that shouldn't have gone missing in the first place.

<StatBlock label="Of assets had no activity in the prior 90 days (idle)" value="{{TODO: ds_idle_asset_rate}}%" />

<StatBlock label="Of Missing assets were recovered via Found-via-Scan within 30 days" value="{{TODO: ds_recovery_rate_found_via_scan}}%" />

Ghost assets are the silent cost. They distort:
- **Budgets** — a ghost asset still shows in inventory totals, inflating the apparent fleet.
- **Depreciation** — a ghost asset still depreciates on paper, even though there's nothing physical to deprecate.
- **Insurance valuations** — a fleet's insured value should reflect what exists, not what records claim.
- **Headcount-per-device ratios** — IT teams that look at "laptops per employee" overstate coverage if ghosts are in the count.

The **{{TODO: ds_recovery_rate_found_via_scan}}%** Found-via-Scan recovery rate is the bright spot. The Shelf workflow lets anyone — even without a Shelf account — scan a QR label on a found item and notify the owner. A non-trivial percentage of Missing assets come back this way, often via members of the public who found the item at a job site, an event venue, or in a rideshare.

The median time to recover a Missing asset is **{{TODO: ds_median_recovery_days}} days**. Outliers go into months. The lesson: a permanent QR label is not just a tracking mechanism — it is a recovery mechanism.

## What top performers do differently

We segmented the sample into top-quartile workspaces (lowest Missing rate, highest on-time return rate, highest audit cadence) and looked for behavioral patterns. Four patterns hold up:

1. **Assign custody within 48 hours of asset creation.** The window matters. Assets created and left without a custodian for more than a week tend to stay uncustomied for the rest of their lifecycle. Top performers have a clear intake ritual: the same person who creates the asset assigns the first custody. Impact: {{TODO: lower missing rate at year-end audit}}.

2. **Audit at least quarterly.** Annual audits surface accumulated drift; quarterly audits prevent drift from accumulating. The marginal cost of an extra audit per year is small — the dedicated audit scan workflow is fast — and the ghost-asset rate is materially lower. Impact: {{TODO: lower ghost-asset rate}}.

3. **Apply QR labels at intake, not retrospectively.** Retroactive labeling programs ("we'll label everything this quarter") almost always end up incomplete. Labels applied at the moment the asset enters inventory are nearly universal in coverage; labels applied later are perpetually catching up. Impact: {{TODO: higher custody coverage}}.

4. **Group multi-component equipment into kits.** This is the single highest-impact behavior for media-and-production and field-ops teams. A camera with three lenses, a tripod, and a battery charger is one kit, not five assets. Kit-level check-out and return is the only mechanism that reliably catches missing accessories before they propagate. Impact: {{TODO: lower missing-accessory rate}}.

All four patterns are practice patterns, not tool patterns. Any organization can adopt them today.

## Limitations and what comes next

No report of this kind is the last word. A few honest caveats:

- **The sample is Shelf customers.** That means the population skews toward teams that have already opted into a structured asset-management practice. Industry-wide rates of, say, ghost assets are probably higher than what we observe, because workspaces that don't use a tool like Shelf don't measure ghosts at all.
- **Self-hosted instances are not in the data.** Shelf is open source and self-hostable. Self-hosted patterns may differ from cloud patterns; we don't have visibility into them and don't try to claim otherwise.
- **One year of data is one year of data.** Year-over-year diffs come into play in the 2027 edition. Some of the numbers in this report are baselines without a comparison point yet.
- **Industry segmentation is best-effort.** We use onboarding-survey responses (`UserBusinessIntel`) to assign workspaces to industries. Workspaces that didn't complete onboarding or that span multiple industries are classified by best-guess. Cohort sizes per industry are disclosed.

The **State of Equipment Management 2027** will be published in the same window next year. If there are specific cuts you'd want us to investigate — by team size, by region, by asset value bracket, by feature usage — [tell us](/contact). We can plan extraction queries against requests.
